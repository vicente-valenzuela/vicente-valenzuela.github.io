Intro

Hello! Welcome to my personal webpage. I am a plasma physicist and currently a Lawrence Fellow at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in California, USA. My main research goal is to connect astrophysical theory, i.e. ideas about the vast cosmos and the structures within, and laboratory experiments done here on Earth.

Please feel free to explore this webpage. To read about my research interests and different project, check out the Research tab. The section Biography explains how I ended up working in this fascinating field and provides a short bio you can use if you ever have to introduce me as a speaker. I have taught different topics at several levels so, if you are a studying or teaching, perhaps you can find something useful in the Tearching Resources tab. Short articles about my publications and other topics can be found in the Blog tab.

Biography

Short bio

I hold a B. Sc. in Physics and an M. Sc. in Experimental Physics from Pontificia Universidad Catolica de Chile. In 2022, I received my Ph. D. in Plasma Physics from Imperial College London where I was a President's Scholar. After, I moved to the Department of Astrophysical Sciences at Princeton University as a Postdoc, where I primarily studied electron heating processes in weakly collisional interactions of laser-driven plasmas. Currently, I am a Lawrence Fellow at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory where I lead a collaboration investigating the instability of black hole accretion disc models through a combination of laboratory experiments, theory, and advanced computational modelling.

Long bio

I was born in Santiago de Chile on October 14 1991, an oddly snowy day of Spring. From a very early age I was fascinated by the natural world, spending most of my time learning about animals, and occassionally inspecting bugs and lizards up close when I could catch them. During my teenage years I struggled with mathematics and was asked several times to do extra hours in the evening at school. However, one day I discovered "analytic" or "cartesian" geometry, which connects algebra and geometry. I was hooked.

Determined to follow a research career in the Natural Sciences, I went into Astronomy. (Originally I wanted to go for the Physics track, but my mother convinced me otherwise... more on that later). The first two years I crashed an burned: the maths were too hard! No worries, perseverance is everything, so I started doing better (but not brilliant). One good summer I got a summer internship in the Plasma Physics laboratory, where I got to repair the Llampudken pulsed-power generator. Llampudken ("butterfly", in the indigenous language Mapudungun), is a pulsed-power driver that produces intense bursts of electrical current we use to create magnetized plasmas from solid rods (they are very thin, so people call them "wires"). The idea was to study how these plasmas flow and evolve, and try and make comparisons with other plasma flows in the universe, such as jets launched during the formation of a star. This sounded way cooler than "traditional" astronomy!
